What are federal student loans and who uses them?

Originally used to promote and enable access to higher education, government-backed student loan programs were first created in 1958 under the National Defense Education Act (NDEA). As history progressed and more efforts are made to encourage education as the way for upward economic and social mobility, the Higher Education Act of 1965 further extended student loans programs. Since then, student loans have been an increasingly large part of U.S. higher education and have evolved to be one of the major sources of financial assistance to students who are seeking postsecondary education.

In present day, students may apply for federal student loan aid through the Free Application for Federal Student Aid (FAFSA). Depending on each student's educational plans (ex: undergraduate or professional student), various grants and loan programs are offered as forms of financial assistance. This project will exclusively analyze the state of student loan programs within the past decade.

It is important to recognize that federal student loan programs are made available through the government, and extension and limitation of such programs are based on presidential and congressional agendas. The U.S. Department of Education oversees the administration and regulations of federal student aid programs, and make these programs accessible for schools to participate in.

Federal Student Loan School Trends: Decreasing Numbers


Data shows that there has been an overall decrease in institutional participation between 2010 to 2019 in federal student loan programs. In 2011 to 2012, the number of institutions participating in loan programs peaked at 5661. By 2019, there were 5162 institutions participating, which accounts for a 9% decrease in school participation numbers.



What types of institutions participate in the federal student loan programs?


Federal student loan programs are made available to both foreign and domestic institutions, given these institutions meet certain program eligibility criteria. By submitting an Electronic Application for Approval to Participate (E-APP) in the Federal Student Financial Aid Programs, schools can apply to be considered eligible to award and disburse various types of federal student aid funding, such as loans, grants, and other federal aid programs.

Each year, schools must also submit a Fiscal Operations Report and Application to Participate (FISAP) to document their expenditures and disbursement data in order to renew their application to participate in such programs.

Federal Student Loan Trends: Proportion of Institutional Participation


From 2010 to 2019, data shows that there is a small pattern emerging in the proportion of school participation. While there wasn't a major proportional change in participation in school participation, data shows that there has been a steady decrease in the proportion of proprietary, for-profit institutions participating in federal student loan programs.

Not surprisingly, domestic schools account for over 90% of institutional participation, and participation in federal student loan programs is not common in foreign institutions.

Data over the past decade shows that there has been steady decreases in participation across most institutional categories, except for foreign proprietary schools.

The largest school count decrease was in the proprietary school category. Reviewing the numbers of proprietary institutions, proprietary school counts decreased from 2078 to 1703, which accounts for an 18% decrease. While it may be hard to attribute the exact reason why proprietary school participation have decreased over the decade, some articles have mentioned an increasing governmental effort to curb federal aid exploitation from proprietary institutions. Additionally, federal regulations surrounding debt to career outcomes ratio such as the Gainful Employment rule aims to prevent schools from misusing federal loan funds.

What types of federal student loans are there?


Icons made by monkik from www.flaticon.com

As students and parents explore different aid options for postsecondary education, affordability and outcomes are at the forefront of discussion. Along with that discussion, federal student loans are also an important part to the college application and decision making process.

These are the types of student loans available through the Federal Student Loan programs:

  • Subsidized Loans: typically made available to undergraduate students with financial need. A major benefit to this type of loan is that interest accruals are deferred when students are at school.
  • Unsubsidized Loans: available to both undergraduate and graduate students and have an annual and lifetime borrowing limit.
  • PLUS Loans: available under Parent PLUS and Graduate PLUS, as a federal loan aid supplement for applicants who are approved based on a credit check.

Note that as of July 2012, subsidized loans are no longer available to graduate students, and students are only eligible to borrow under unsubsidized and PLUS loan funds.

How has federal student loan debt evolved over the past decade?

From changes in federal regulations to discussions of skyrocketing education costs, the student loan landscape has evolved significantly over the past decade.

Data from the past decade indicates that the number of loan recipients and amount of loans disbursed at the undergraduate level has decreased. The average debt per student has remained constant, partly due to federally yearly loan maximums.

Loans at the graduate level, such as the Graduate Unsubsidized and PLUS loans have increased incrementally over the years. After subsidized loans have been phased out at the graduate level, students had to rely more on the Unsubsidized and PLUS loans to keep up with tuition and living costs, which has also increased over the past decade. Additionally, Parent PLUS loans have increased about $4,000 on average across different schools.
